Education

Quantum physics is possibly one of the most difficult subjects to understand in the entire universe. It is so fundamentally weird and unorthodox in nature that we don’t completely understand it yet, and it has even stumped some of the brightest minds in history. Although we don’t understand most of the reasoning behind a lot of quantum mechanics, we know they exist and we use them and study them. This is why even though most of how a quantum computer works will not be understood, we know they will work because we have studied how we can use quantum properties to make them work. The quantum world is one that creates the basis of our universe, explaining the tiniest things in existence and how they behave in reaction to things like matter and forces. Since particles are so tiny, it is almost impossible to study what goes on in the quantum world. This is why some quantum properties scare us, make us question all of classical physics, and disturbs us about the true nature of reality. But we use them to our advantage the best we can. Here are a few of them that help explain quantum computers better.

Quantum Physics Concepts Used

Qubits

Qubits are subatomic particles such as electrons or photons that replace classical bits for the smallest units of information for a quantum computer. Their properties are measured to produce values of 0 or 1 as binary digits do as well. However, quantum properties of these particles allow them to have more computing power than can ever be possible on a regular computer. Two of these properties are called superposition and entanglement.

Superposition

While classical bits can either be 0 or 1 at any time, qubits can be 0 and 1 simultaneously. The ability to be in multiple states at the same time is called superposition. The reason this can happen all comes down to how particles behave at the quantum level. They behave like waves. Think of it like this. There are two waves on the ocean. Each is distinct in their own state and form. However, they can come together and overlap each other to form a new bigger wave. This, itself, is also its own distinct state and form. Another key thing is that when qubits are measured for their information, they will “collapse” to either one of the states 0 and 1. This means their final calculation results snap back to normal states we can observe, but only when they are done calculating. If the new big wave is both tiny waves at the same time, once we surf on it, it will immediately turn into one of the two smaller waves. If it sounds confusing, that’s because it is.

Entanglement

A pair of qubits that exist in a single quantum state are said to be “entangled.” The traits of this property are phenomenally weird but interesting. If the state of one qubit is changed, it will instantaneously change the other’s state, no matter how far apart the two are. The furthest scientists have tested the distance this works is a few hundred kilometres, but theoretically, there is no limit. Yes, even light years away this will happen. This also means that if we interact with one entangled qubit, we can easily predict or deduce the properties of the other entangled qubit. The explanation for this quantum property is not quite so simple, for it has even baffled scientists themselves. Even Einstein called it “spooky action at a distance.” Quantum computers also depend on this property to exponentially increase their computing power over classical computers. The ability to instantaneously know the outcome of entangled qubits allows it to increase the speed of its calculations with specially designed quantum algorithms.

Decoherence

There is a quantum property that creates a drawback for quantum computers, though. Qubits aren’t perfect, and how they react with the environment causes possibilities for them to lose their quantum state, and therefore the quantum properties that make the computer work. This is called decoherence. Since their quantum state is extremely fragile, tiny changes in vibrations or temperature may cause the qubits to lose their quantum states. This is the reason why quantum computers have huge chambers that supercool them or suspend them in an isolated vacuum. They must protect the qubits from the outside world from interrupting their work before they are done. This is a big problem that hinders the progress of quantum computers, which scientists are primarily figuring out with smart quantum algorithms to prevent them or by simply making more qubits. However, generating qubits is hard, and scientists haven’t been able to generate more than 128 standard ones yet (although IBM claims they will make a quantum computer with over 1000 by 2023). This is why no quantum computer can defeat the best supercomputers yet. Unless the problems with decoherence and the ability to generate more qubits gets tackled and perhaps solved later on, the era of quantum computing will be delayed.

Quantum Gates

In classical computers, logic gates are data processors that take a set of inputs and give back one definite output. All parts of the process are conspicuous and literal. However, a quantum gate is a logic gate of a quantum computer that works differently due to qubit manipulation. The input a quantum gate takes in is a bunch of superpositions, and as the function happens all it does inside is rotate probabilities of the superpositions, and then the output is also a bunch of superpositions, but with changed probabilities of outcomes for the qubits to become 0 or 1. A quantum computer basically sets up qubits and uses quantum gates to entangle them and manipulate their quantum properties, and then it finalizes them into a string of 0s and 1s we can read as the answer. What does this mean? It means we can calculate all the possible calculations we can do with the inputs all at once. Imagine if you have a set of data that you need to go through the logic gate, and you need each input to get each output one after the other. Instead, you can throw all the inputs in the quantum gate at once and it will manipulate them to get all the outputs. Unlike classical logic gates, quantum gates are even reversible, and just as logic gates are to conventional computers, they are the building blocks of quantum circuits in a quantum computer. Classical bits are either 0 or 1, therefore representing their values results in just saying 0 or 1. However, since qubits are in superposition, you must represent their values in a matrix (plural matrices). The identity matrix is the base value of a qubit and it is depicted as an array of I = [[1,0][0,1]]. 1 and 0 at the same time or 0 and 1 at the same time. Of course, this is only before they are measured. Otherwise, when observed, they will immediately choose to be 0 or 1 in a definite manner.

Quantum Tunneling

Quantum tunneling isn’t a property that makes quantum computers work, however it is one that has caused us to try to create them due to problems it causes with classical computers. It is the phenomenon of subatomic particles acting like waves and passing through barriers. When our transistors get too small and this starts happening with electrons, it messes up our computers and the information they had could be lost forever. It is one of the reasons our classical computers cannot get any smaller than they are already and therefore cannot fit more transistors to become more powerful. How does it happen? In classical physics, a ball requires energy to be pushed up and over a hill. However, in quantum physics, the rules are different. Think about it, we're talking about the smallest things in existence. Within atoms themselves is mostly empty space (no matter), and if things are small enough, like let's say an electron, there's always a probability they can squeeze through that empty space. It's just when we observe things on the human-scale, we think it's impossible to phase through solid matter because of how big we are and how tightly packed atoms are in our perspective and world. Quantum tunneling has been observed in things such as radioactive decay and is real, defined by the probability subatomic particles acting like waves has to "tunnel" through matter.

The Steps of a Quantum Computer

  1. Generate qubits
  2. Suspend qubits in a quantum state
  3. Use qubits in quantum gates
  4. Perform calculations with quantum gate circuits
  5. Measure the qubits for the final output

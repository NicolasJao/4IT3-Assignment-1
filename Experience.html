Applications

As a matter of fact, the reliance on quantum computers has risen greatly ever since the beginning of the pandemic. Some companies have used quantum computing to better process the huge amounts of data of the COVID-19 pandemic than regular computers. Since pandemics are very complex with the nature of how they spread and affect supply and demand of business and much more, this is an interesting application. Other than that, quantum computing is surprisingly useful in a variety of areas and industries. Here are just a few interesting ones.

Database Searching

Have you ever gone to a celebrity’s profile on social media and searched through their millions of followers the name of someone in order to find out if they follow them? You might get no results, even if it’s not true, and they do follow that celebrity. Database searching is a tedious process for a classical computer, as it takes the characters that you’ve inputted and has to go through every single one in its database to check for a match. Now imagine if it has to go through millions, just like the social media example. With the exponential processing power of a quantum computer, it can find what you’re looking for in a huge database much, much faster, at an estimated square root of the original time.

Solving Famously-Difficult Mathematical Problems

A funny and popular use for quantum computers is one not quite so noble. It involves ruining security system technologies currently being used by practically the entire world, destroying privacy as we know it. Data such as browsing history, emails, banking data, credit card details, and other personal classified information is kept secure by an encryption system that keeps it all secure by you owning a public key that only you can decode. Decrypting it is so insanely difficult because they involve gigantic prime numbers that need to be factorized. This is related to the P vs NP problem, a famously difficult mathematical problem that still hasn’t been solved today. In a nutshell, the question posed is, “Once you have the answer to a hard problem, it is easy to quickly check if it is right. But does this also mean that the problem itself can also be solved just as quickly?” Problems in NP have answers that are easy to check. A subcategory in NP is P, which are problems that have answers that are easy to check, and they are fairly quick to find the answer for. The question is, does P = NP? Are all problems in NP, in fact, also in P? Or are there problems that are truly so hard that we can check an answer easily if they're correct for them, but we don't have any good way of solving the problem quickly? For example, checking if two prime numbers are factors of another big prime is easy. Just multiply them and check. However, does this also mean finding those two factors is just as easy when given only the big prime? As we already know, seemingly it’s no. Which is why encryption systems around the world still work. Safety in numbers. But we cannot know for sure. A quantum computer can challenge that idea and finally solve the P vs NP problem (if you solve it you get a million dollars from the Clay Institute of Mathematics). Even if you build faster computers to factorize these numbers, it can be easily countered by the system just multiplying bigger numbers into even larger prime numbers. A quantum computer, however, can use a factorization method so devastatingly fast due to the way it computes things (all at once) that their invention could spark a security information crisis. Imagine an organization getting their hands on a quantum computer to decrypt all the world’s secrets! Using its power to transfer money from bank accounts to theirs! Decrypting nuclear launch codes! Quantum computers have the possibility to be extremely dangerous as much as revolutionary.

Complex Simulations

There is a saying that goes something like, “A flap of a butterfly’s wings in (insert place here) can cause a tornado in (insert another faraway place here).” This is based on chaos theory, a mathematical theory that’s fairly complex in its own right because it tries to explain the nature of complex systems such as our planet. That tiny things like butterflies can cause huge changes to the environment that are drastically different from initial conditions through complicated processes. It’s the reason why weather is sometimes so unpredictable, no matter how good our technology is. With the computational power of quantum computers, perhaps we can tackle this challenge head-on by simulating them with one. Imagine a quantum computer simulating an environmental weather system so complicated and down to every last detail that it can predict if a certain butterfly’s flap of the wings will cause it to rain today. This is an aim quantum computers are being made for, and while we haven’t done any huge-scale simulations like this yet, theoretically they’d be able to. They’d be able to simulate the quantum world so we can better understand the history and nature of the universe, perhaps tying in Einstein’s general theory of relativity with our current understanding of space. They’d be able to model complex molecules and proteins with incredible accuracy that could revolutionize medicine and innovate health technology. They’d be able to help astronauts simulate rockets and gravity in space and therefore aid in space exploration. The possibilities are endless. In theory, this would be the greatest purpose of quantum computers.

Note: Quantum computers are only faster than classical computers when doing tasks that require numerous calculations to be done in parallel. If you want to watch puppy or kitten videos on YouTube or write an essay, classical computers are much better suited for the task. However, if you want to find one item in a list of a million items, a quantum computer can do so exponentially faster than a classical computer who would have to analyze each item one by one.

Companies

Many tech companies are investing and researching quantum computers, pushing the frontiers of the field further than ever before. They are moving it out of the academic world and into the world of application, turning it from a theoretical science into usable and efficient technology. As quantum computers advance faster and faster, only time will tell if they will prove to be worthy of their dreamed-of computational impeccability. Here are a few companies pursuing this dauntless task.

Atom Computing

Founded in 2018, Atom Computing creates quantum computer hardware. They specialize in neutral atom quantum computers (using atom energy levels for qubits). Their projects are still mostly prototypes. Ben Bloom, founder and CEO of the company, said it will provide cloud access "to large numbers of very coherent qubits by optically trapping and addressing individual atoms.”

Xanadu

Xanadu is a Canadian quantum computing technology company. They aim to build quantum computers that are useful and available to people everywhere, to commercialize and normalize it. It was founded in 2016. They want to build a universal quantum computer using silicon photonic hardware, according to Sepehr Taghavi, corporate development manager. They also have a Xanadu Quantum Cloud (XQC) service, hosting a server where people can use their quantum computers for application purposes.

IBM

Yes, one of the most well-known computer parts manufacturers and literally the world’s oldest computer company also focuses a lot on developing quantum computing technology. In 2016, they were the first company to put a quantum computer on the cloud with 260,000 registered users. IBM scientists aim to create a quantum computer with a 1,121 qubit processor by 2023, which will be a 10 foot tall “super fridge.” According to the company this will be capable of delivering a “Quantum Advantage,” a point where it can do tasks more efficiently than a classical supercomputer. Essentially they mean to prove quantum supremacy.

Google

In 2019, Google along with NASA and Oak Ridge National Laboratory claimed to achieve quantum supremacy by calculating something in seconds that even the most advanced supercomputers would take thousands of years to do. This is a huge milestone for the advancement of the information age and could revolutionize computers forever. However it was met with a lot of controversy whether they truly reached quantum supremacy or not. A lot of the feud happened with IBM, a big rival of Google when it comes to commercializing quantum computing. IBM questioned their achievement and whether it was really that big or not.

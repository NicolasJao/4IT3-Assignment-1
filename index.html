The (Possible) Solution

The answer is Quantum Computing. It is an experimental but very scientifically real idea that uses specific properties and concepts of quantum physics to perform tasks of orders of magnitude much greater than any classical computer. The machines that use quantum computing are called quantum computers. Though complex, with enough research and testing, quantum computers are very quickly leaving the world of science fiction and entering reality. These supposed supercomputers will be able to simulate weather with unbelievable accuracy, model molecular structures down to each protein, calculate deep space travel routes while incorporating all possible scenarios. But what exactly are they? How do they use quantum physics to do things classical computers can’t? How do they work? For that, we’re going to need a little bit of a history lesson about computers.

Classical Computers

“Classical computers” here will refer mostly to computers as they are today. Classical computers are made up of a bunch of many tiny parts that do different simple tasks, which altogether combine to do practically anything. For example, a circuit chip is made up of modules, each module has logic gates, and each logic gate consists of the simplest form of a data processor in a computer, transistors. What is a transistor, and what is its job? A transistor is basically a switch that controls the flow of data or information which comes in the form of a bunch of electrons. Since a transistor can be in two distinct states, open and closed, it can give two types of information, which machines read as either 0 or 1, called bits. This is where we hit our problem. Transistors are already reaching the limits of the smallest they can be. As mentioned previously, some are capable of being as small as a single atom. And on top of that, if transistors get too small, electrons might be capable of doing a process called “Quantum Tunneling” where they simply phase through the gate of the transistor, messing up the system and ruining the information, corrupting the data forever. In order to reach higher levels of computing power, we can’t look any further into hardware. Instead, we must look into the quantum realm, the world of the tiniest things in existence and the strange nature of how they work: particles. Quantum computers promise to solve this problem of hardware limitation by changing the bits of information being used themselves. Instead of using classical bits which can either be 0 or 1, quantum computers use qubits, which can be both 0 and 1 at the same time! This raises the amount of traditional computing power exponentially. How? Quantum computers are able to calculate many different outcomes to a problem at the same time, while classical computers have to do so one at a time, one after the other. This is why no matter how fast you can make a classical computer at doing its tasks, quantum computers will always be faster. For example, let’s perceive a string of four bits, each one either 0 or 1. The string of four bits, also called a “nibble,” can have 2^4 possible different values, in other words, 16. And at any given moment, you can only use one of them, since classical bits can ever only be either 0 or 1. This is where the magic of qubits comes in. If qubits can be 0 and 1 at the same time (this is done via a process called superposition), only becoming either one of them when we need them to, then we can use all 16 possibilities of a nibble at once. Suddenly, our computing power is 16 times more powerful than before. Finally, you can have two chrome tabs open at once!

Information

Starting from the middle of the twentieth century, we are currently in what is widely known as the “Information Age.” What describes our current era of humanity and civilization is information and how we interact with it. Digital media, technology, news, the internet, and our access to all of it is a defining characteristic of our generation. It is said that the Information Age is very closely related to the development of transistor technology. Why is that? In fact, what is information? At least, the one we’re talking about? At its very core, information is anything that can resemble something that can be interpreted and manipulated into what we want it to do or say. In computers, bits are the tiniest pieces of information, but physically they consist of electrons running through transistors controlling their flow. In a nutshell, transistors control their flow and therefore voltage, and different levels of voltage give a signal of two values. In the case of bits, these two values are 0 and 1. These values of 0 and 1 are then interpreted by the computer through their own processing language called machine code, the technically lowest-level programming language. Machine code is the language that our compilers turn our programming code into when we program computers with languages we actually can work with and understand such as Python and Java. However, information doesn’t have to come in the physical form of electrons and their voltage. In quantum computers, information still comes in two values, but they come in the physical form as the properties of particles, such as the spin and magnetic field of a subatomic particle, or a photon’s horizontal and vertical polarization. These properties are measured by the quantum computer, processed as machine code, and converted into whatever task the computer needs to do. That’s where the possibilities of quantum physics come in. In the quantum world of particles, the rules of physics are weirder than ever before. For example, an electron orbiting an atom has a position and a momentum. However, due to the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle, you cannot measure both at the same time. If you locate its position, you cannot know its momentum, and if you measure its momentum, you cannot know its position. So, instead, electrons exist in a cloud of probability of where they could be. Scientifically, these are called orbitals. This two-way quantum system is similar to superposition which is why qubits can be two distinct states at the same time, which will be explained later. Instead of being limited by the rules of the quantum world by not being able to make computers any smaller, scientists are using the rules and properties of quantum physics to their advantage. These rules and properties will be explained further in the second page of this website, in, “How They Work.”

Quantum Supremacy

As you know now, quantum computers use the properties of particles, their nature, and quantum physics to perform tasks much better and faster than any classical computer. Right now, it’s a very experimental subject that still needs further research on. However, it is quickly becoming viable and will definitely be a technology that will exist in the near future. The hardest part of creating a quantum computer is generating qubits. As mentioned before, qubits exist physically as subatomic particles with properties we measure that can be converted into computer information. Some companies cool circuits to temperatures near absolute zero to create them. Some trap atoms in magnetic fields in ultra-vacuum chambers. This is why pictures you see of quantum computers are typically huge, wiry, complicated messes and chambers. They look really complicated, but the goal is simple. In the end the goal of a quantum computer is to isolate the qubits in a controlled quantum state that we can manipulate and measure. Okay, maybe not so simple. The idea of quantum supremacy is proving that quantum computers will be able to perform tasks classical computers can’t. As of right now, there isn’t a task given to them that classical computers can’t do. But as they get better and better, quantum computers will surely be able to beat their conventional counterparts. Will they replace regular computers? It’s hard to say. Some scientists think they will, and they are the future of computational technology. Others think we’ll mostly still use classical computers, and quantum computers will be specialized for the super hard computational tasks, such as simulating environments as big as a planet to as small as a human body, or helping astronauts travel deep space. Though quantum computers look vastly different than traditional computers, sharing practically no hardware or engineering at all, they both fundamentally function the same: aiding humanity in calculating tasks that our monkey brains would not be able to do themselves. It’s just, out of the two, quantum computers will be much better and faster at it.

Pros and Cons of Quantum Computers
Pros Cons
  • Exponentially faster at calculations that need to be done in parallel
  • Potential to revolutionize research of practically anything
  • May allow almost unlimited computing power for whatever purpose
  • Will help us understand the nature of reality better, the quantum world
  • Not faster at simple tasks of everyday computers
  • Hard to create and sustain qubits
  • Hard to make and expensive hardware
  • People might use them to hack unhackable things
